Equivalent Norms And $\ell^p$ Spaces

Equivalent Norms

Two norms, $||\cdot||_{1}, ||\cdot||_{2}$, are said to be equivalent if and only if there exist constants $0 < c_1 \leq c_2$ such that for all $x \in X$ $$c_1 ||x||_{1} \leq ||x||_{2} \leq c_2 ||x||_{1}$$

Alternatively, this can also be written as two norms, $||\cdot||_{1}, ||\cdot||_{2}$, are said to be equivalent if and only if there exist constants $0 < c_1 \leq c_2$ such that $$c_1 \mathcal{B}_{(X, ||\cdot||_2)} \subset \mathcal{B}_{(X, ||\cdot||_1)} \subset c_2 \mathcal{B}_{(X, ||\cdot||_2)}$$

The $\ell^p$ Sequence Space

For $ 1 \leq p < \infty$, the $\ell^p$ Sequence Space is defined as the space of all sequences $x = (x_j)_{j=1}^{\infty}$ such that $$\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}|x_j|^p < \infty$$

The $\ell^{\infty}$ Sequence Space is defined as the space of bounded sequences equipped with the $\ell^\infty$ Norm.

Proof that the $\ell^p$ Sequence Space is closed under addition is provided later. A full proof of $\ell^p$ as a vector space is not provided.

Minkowski's Inequality In $\ell^p$

Minkowski's Inequality In $\ell^p$ states that for all $ 1 \leq p \leq \infty$, if $x,y\in\ell^p$, $x + y \in \ell^p$ and $||x + y||_{\ell^p} \leq ||x||_{\ell^p} + ||y||_{\ell^p}$.

Proving the case for $\ell^\infty$ is simple. For $x,y \in \ell^{\infty}$, $$||x+y||_{\ell^\infty} = \max_{j\in{[1,\infty)}}|x_j + y_j|$$ $$\implies ||x+y||_{\ell^\infty} = \max_{j\in{[1,\infty)}}|x_j| + \max_{j\in{[1,\infty)}}|y_j||$$ $$\implies ||x+y||_{\ell^{\infty}} = ||x||_{\ell^\infty} + ||y||_{\ell^\infty}$$

Therefore Minkowski's Inequality holds for $\ell^\infty$.

For the cases of $1 \leq p < \infty$, consider Minkowski's Inequality In $\mathbb{R}^n$ (seen in The $\ell^p$ Norms). For $x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n$, and $1 \leq p < \infty$, $$||x+y||_{\ell^p} \leq ||x||_{\ell^p} + ||y||_{\ell^p}$$ $$\implies \left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_j+y_j|^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}} = \left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_j|^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}} + \left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}|y_j|^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}}$$

Since $\ell^p$ infinite dimensional and the series of elements of $\ell^p$ are finite, we can take the limit as $n\rightarrow\infty$, meaning that $$\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_j+y_j|^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}} = \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_j|^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}} + \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}|y_j|^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}}$$ $$\implies ||x+y||_{\ell^p} \leq ||x||_{\ell^p} + ||y||_{\ell^p}$$

For all $x,y\in \ell^p$, and thus Minkowski's Inequality holds for $1 \leq p < \infty$. Finally, we can see that for all $1 \leq p \leq \infty$, Minkowski's Inequality In $\ell^p$ holds.

Proof That $\ell^p$ Is Closed Under Addition

To prove that $\ell^p$ is closed under addition, for $1 \leq p < \infty$ and $x,y \in \ell^p$, we have that $$\sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_j + y_j|^p \leq \sum_{j=1}^{n}|2\max\{x_j, y_j\}|^p$$ $$\implies \sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_j + y_j|^p \leq 2^p\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}|\max\{x_j^p, y_j^p\}|\right)$$ $$\implies \sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_j + y_j|^p \leq 2^p\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_j^p| + \sum_{j=1}^{n}|y_j^p|\right)$$

Taking the limit as $n\rightarrow\infty$, we see that $$\implies \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_j + y_j|^p \leq \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}2^p\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_j^p| + \sum_{j=1}^{n}|y_j^p|\right)$$

Since the two sums on the right are finite (as $x,y\in \ell^p$), the left side must also be finite, and thus $\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}|x_j+y_j|^p < \infty$ and thus $x+y \in \ell^p$ if $x,y \in\ell^p$ for $1 \leq p < \infty$.

All other conditions are trivial to prove (Additive Commutativity, Associativity, Inverse, and Identity, Multiplicative Closure, Distributivity, and Identity), including the additive closure in the case of $\ell^\infty$.
